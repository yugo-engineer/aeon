# Paper Digest — 2026-03-10

## Consciousness & Neuroscience

1. **What a "Landscape of Consciousness" Means for Neurology and Neuroscience** — Robert Lawrence Kuhn (2025)
   Rather than rallying behind a single theory of consciousness, Kuhn catalogues over 350 explanations across 10 primary categories (with materialism alone spanning 12 subcategories), building the most comprehensive taxonomy of consciousness theories to date. The paper argues that clinical neurology and psychiatry can benefit from this theoretical plurality — questions about AI sentience, free will, and personal identity cannot be meaningfully addressed without situating them within a specific consciousness framework. The taxonomy also highlights how many open questions remain genuinely unresolved, cautioning against premature theoretical closure.
   [Link](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12718250/) | Open Access

2. **Serotonin Modulation of Consciousness and Perception** — Callum M. White et al. (2026)
   Published in *Nature Communications* (January 2026), this study investigates how serotonin modulates conscious perception by enhancing top-down predictive processing. The findings suggest that serotonergic signaling doesn't just alter mood — it fundamentally reshapes how the brain constructs conscious experience from sensory input, offering new mechanistic links between psychedelic pharmacology and theories of predictive coding. This work bridges the gap between molecular neuropharmacology and consciousness science in a way that could inform both clinical psychedelic therapy and basic consciousness research.
   [Link](https://www.nature.com/subjects/consciousness)

## Brain-Computer Interfaces

3. **Non-Invasive Brain-Computer Interfaces: Converging Frontiers in Neural Signal Decoding and Flexible Bioelectronics Integration** — *Nano-Micro Letters* (2026)
   This comprehensive review maps the convergence of deep learning-based neural decoding with advances in flexible and stretchable electrode materials for non-invasive BCIs. The authors identify multimodal data fusion, hardware-software co-optimization, and closed-loop control strategies as the three critical pillars for making non-invasive BCIs robust enough for real-world use. The paper is notable for arguing that wearable BCI systems may reach clinical utility faster than invasive approaches if these engineering challenges are solved in tandem.
   [Link](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s40820-025-02042-2) | Open Access

4. **Memristor-Based Neuromorphic BCI Decoder** — University of Hong Kong, Tsinghua University, Tianjin University (2026)
   Published in *Nature Electronics*, this paper demonstrates a memristor-based brain-computer interface decoder that achieved 85.17% accuracy in a real-world drone flight control task while consuming 1,643x less energy and operating 216x faster (normalized) than conventional CPU-based systems. The neuromorphic approach sidesteps the power and latency bottlenecks that have limited portable BCI applications, suggesting a path toward truly untethered brain-controlled devices. This is one of the first demonstrations of memristive hardware achieving competitive decoding accuracy in a practical BCI application.
   [Link](https://www.sciencedaily.com/releases/2025/12/251209234139.htm)

## AI Agents & Large Language Models

5. **Agentic Large Language Models, a Survey** — Aske Plaat, Max van Duijn, Niki van Stein, Mike Preuss, Peter van der Putten, Kees Joost Batenburg (2025)
   Published in the *Journal of Artificial Intelligence Research* (Vol. 84), this survey organizes the rapidly expanding field of LLM agents into three reinforcing pillars: reasoning (reflection, retrieval, planning), action (tool use, robotics, real-world interaction), and interaction (multi-agent collaboration, emergent social behavior). A key insight is that these capabilities are deeply intertwined — retrieval enables tool use, reflection improves multi-agent collaboration, and reasoning benefits all categories. The authors flag unresolved safety, liability, and security concerns as the primary barriers to real-world deployment, while identifying medical diagnosis, logistics, and financial analysis as the most promising near-term applications.
   [Link](https://arxiv.org/abs/2503.23037) | Open Access
